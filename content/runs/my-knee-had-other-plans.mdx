# My Knee Had Other Plans (And What I Did About It)

I'll skip the dramatic setup. Here's what happened: I had an accident. Broke my backbone, tibia, and femur. Three bones that, when they break together, tend to end the conversation about running before it starts.

Doctors were clear. Physios were clear. My body, lying in a hospital bed held together by metal and optimism, was extremely clear. Running was not on the menu. Probably ever.

That was the plan. Mine was different.

## The Part Nobody Prepares You For

The injuries heal — slowly, painfully, boringly — but they heal. What nobody warns you about is the in-between. The phase where you're technically "recovered" but your body doesn't trust itself anymore. Your legs work but your brain keeps sending memos about all the things that could go wrong.

My knee became the loudest voice in that committee. Every step had an opinion. Going downstairs felt like a negotiation. And Malta, in case you haven't noticed, is basically made of stairs.

The official diagnosis was patellofemoral pain syndrome — runner's knee. Which felt almost insulting given that I wasn't even running yet. I was just existing with stairs.

## The Comeback Nobody Clapped For

There was no montage. No moment where the music swells and I sprint along the Sliema seafront looking powerful. The comeback was slow, boring, and full of setbacks that would make a terrible Instagram story.

Week one: walking without limping. That was it. That was the whole achievement.

Week four: walking uphill without stopping to swear. Progress.

Month three: a slow jog — maybe 500 metres — along the flat part of the promenade. My pace was embarrassing. My knee was complaining. But I was moving forward, and that mattered more than any number on a watch.

## What Actually Helped (The Boring List)

**Strength work I'd never done before.** Turns out runner's knee is often a hip and glute problem in disguise. When those muscles aren't pulling their weight, the knee takes all the load. Lateral band walks and single-leg squats aren't glamorous. They work.

**A physio who didn't treat me like I was fragile.** Most medical professionals, when they hear your injury list, want to wrap you in cotton wool. I needed someone who'd say "yes, this will hurt, and you're doing it anyway." Found one. Changed everything.

**Running slower than my ego wanted.** I spent weeks at a pace that felt almost embarrassingly slow. But when you slow down, your form cleans up, you land softer, and everything has a bit more time to do its job. My ego survived. My knee thanked me.

**The foam roller, taken seriously for the first time.** I owned one for years. It lived under my bed doing nothing. Once I actually committed to rolling my quads and IT band daily, I understood why everyone talks about it. Unpleasant in the useful way.

## The Bit Where It Clicked

I don't remember the exact run. I wish I did — it would make a better story. But somewhere around the fifth month, I was running along the coast near Dingli Cliffs and I realised I hadn't thought about my knee for the past kilometre.

Not because it was numb or because I was ignoring it. Because it was fine. Just... fine.

That was the real return. Not a personal best, not a race finish. Just a kilometre where my body did what I asked without arguing back.

## If You're Coming Back From Something

Go see a physio. Not Google at 11pm — an actual human who can look at how you move and tell you what's weak. I wasted weeks on guesswork that could've been sorted in two appointments.

Rest properly. Not the "I'll just do an easy 5k" kind of rest. The actual boring kind where you do your exercises and you wait.

And when you do start again: go slower than you think you need to. Your cardiovascular fitness drops faster than your leg strength, so your legs feel ready before your lungs agree. That's normal. It comes back quicker than you'd think.

The bones healed. The knee cooperated eventually. And now I run races in Malta and complain about hills like everyone else. Allura — if that's not a comeback, I don't know what is.
