Here's what Gozo looks like in July if you go to Ramla Bay at 11am: beautiful, crowded, loud, everything sticky with sunscreen and nowhere comfortable to put your towel.

Here's what Gozo looks like at the same time, twenty minutes away on foot: rust-coloured sand, water so clear you can count the rocks at the bottom, maybe eight other people, and silence except for the sea.

That place is San Blas Bay. And the reason most people never find it is exactly why you should go.

## Why San Blas Stays Secret

There are no kiosks. No sun lounger rentals. No road that gets you close enough to make it easy. The path down is steep — genuinely steep, the kind that requires actual concentration going down and real effort coming back up, especially in summer heat. Tour buses cannot reach it. People who want convenience won't bother.

Which means what's left is one of the most beautiful bays in Gozo for the people willing to do a proper 15-minute descent on a rocky path to reach it.

I'll take that deal every time.

![Red sand and turquoise Mediterranean water](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1519046904884-53103b34b206?w=1200&q=80)

## What You'll Actually Find There

The sand is the first thing that catches you off guard. It's deep red-orange — the colour of terracotta, of the Gozitan countryside that meets the sea here. Against turquoise water, the effect is almost unreal. The kind of thing you'd suspect was edited if you weren't standing in it.

The bay is small and protected by high cliffs on either side, which keeps it calm even when the coast has a swell. The water is shallow for a good stretch before it deepens — great for swimming without drama. Snorkelling is worth it on a clear day. Visibility here is exceptional.

What there isn't: shade, facilities, food, or any reason to linger if you haven't brought your own supplies. Bring everything.

## How To Get There

From Victoria (Gozo's capital), take the road toward Nadur. From Nadur, follow signs toward San Blas — there's a small car park at the top of the valley where the road ends. From there it's on foot: a winding path down through terraced fields and carob trees, about 15 minutes to reach the beach.

**The hill warning:** The path back up is steep and there's no shade in the valley. If you're going in summer, go early — before 10am — or late afternoon. Walking that climb at midday in August is genuinely unpleasant. You've been warned.

![Steep Mediterranean path to beach](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1473116763249-2faaef81ccda?w=1200&q=80)

**Getting to Gozo from Malta:** The ferry leaves from Cirkewwa in the north of Malta, takes 25 minutes, runs frequently. Foot passengers don't need to book ahead. Bring a car if you can — getting around Gozo without one limits you.

## The Best Time To Go

**Early morning** is the move. The light on the red sand before 9am is something else, and you'll likely have the bay to yourself.

**Late September and October** are genuinely underrated. Sea is still warm from summer, crowds are largely gone, and the countryside on the walk down turns golden.

**Avoid** peak August midday unless crowds don't bother you and you enjoy suffering uphill in full sun.

## What To Bring (Non-Negotiable)

- Water. More than you think. The descent uses it and the climb back definitely does.
- Food — there is nothing to buy at the beach.
- Reef-safe sunscreen. The bay is pristine; help it stay that way.
- Snorkel mask if you have one.
- A bag for your rubbish. Leave it as you found it.
- Proper sandals or trainers for the path.

![Clear Mediterranean snorkelling water](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1560275619-4662e36fa65c?w=1200&q=80)

## Why It's Worth The Effort

There's a certain kind of travel satisfaction that only comes from places that ask something of you to get there — even when "something" is just a steep downhill walk and the foresight to pack lunch.

When you get to the top on the way back and turn around one last time, you'll understand exactly why it was worth it.
