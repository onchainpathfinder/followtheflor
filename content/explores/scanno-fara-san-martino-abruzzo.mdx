There's a moment on the road between Villalago and Scanno where you come around a bend and the lake appears below — turquoise water filling a valley between mountains, shaped like something you can't quite place. Then someone tells you it's heart-shaped when seen from above, and suddenly Abruzzo feels like it's showing off.

Scanno is the village everyone in Abruzzo tells you to visit. Fara San Martino is the one nobody mentions unless they're serious about hiking. And Pietracupa — well, Pietracupa is a place where a village decided to build itself around a rock the size of a cathedral and just carry on.

Three stops, one day, zero regrets.

<Callout type="insight" title="The Abruzzo triangle">
Scanno, Fara San Martino, and Pietracupa form a rough triangle across the heart of Abruzzo and into Molise. You can hit all three in a long day from Sulmona, but giving each place a morning or afternoon lets you actually breathe.
</Callout>

## Scanno: The Village With the Heart-Shaped Lake

Scanno sits on a steep hillside, houses stacked on top of each other, dominated by a massive rock formation with a cross on top. It's been called one of the most beautiful villages in Italy, and for once the hype is earned.

![Scanno village perched on a hillside with an enormous rock formation rising above it, topped with a cross](/images/explores/abruzzo/scanno-village.jpg)
*Scanno from the approach road. The rock above the village looks like it's about to make an announcement.*

The village itself is a maze of narrow alleys, stone arches, and old women in doorways. There's a goldsmith tradition here that goes back centuries, and you'll find tiny workshops selling filigree jewellery unique to Scanno.

But the real draw is the lake. **Lago di Scanno** sits in the valley below — formed by a landslide thousands of years ago that dammed the Sagittario river. From certain viewpoints, its shape is unmistakably a heart.

![A heart-shaped flower arch at a viewpoint in Scanno, framing green mountains and the valley below](/images/explores/abruzzo/scanno-heart.jpg)
*The mandatory heart photo. They put the arch here because they know exactly what you came for.*

I kayaked it. Rented a kayak from the small beach area and paddled across water so clear you can see the bottom at five metres. The mountains rise on both sides, the village watches from above, and the only sound is your paddle breaking the surface.

![Flor kayaking on Lago di Scanno in an orange life vest, mountains and willow trees reflected in the calm water](/images/explores/abruzzo/scanno-kayak.jpg)
*Kayaking Lago di Scanno. The water was so still it felt wrong to disturb it.*

<StatHighlight value="922m" label="Altitude of Lago di Scanno — the largest natural lake in Abruzzo" accent="pink" />

<Callout type="tip" title="Best viewpoint for the heart shape">
Drive or hike up to the viewpoint on the road between Scanno and Villalago (SP479). There's a signed pullover with the classic aerial view. Go in the morning for the best light on the water.
</Callout>

## Fara San Martino: Where Pasta Meets the Gorge

Thirty minutes east of Scanno, the Majella mountains drop into a gorge so narrow you can touch both walls with your arms outstretched. This is the **Gole di Fara San Martino** — and the reason your De Cecco pasta exists.

![Flor standing in the gorge of Fara San Martino, dwarfed by towering limestone walls rising on either side](/images/explores/abruzzo/fara-san-martino-gorge.jpg)
*Scale check. Those walls are about 200 metres high. I am not.*

Fara San Martino is a small town at the foot of the Majella, and the spring water that flows from this gorge is what De Cecco and Del Verde use to make their pasta. The mineral content, the temperature, the purity — it's not marketing, it's geology. The pasta factories sit right at the mouth of the gorge, drawing water directly from the source.

The hike into the gorge follows the river bed through increasingly narrow passages until you reach the ruins of **San Martino in Valle** — a Benedictine monastery built directly into the rock face in the 9th century. The monks chose this spot for the same reason the pasta makers did: the water.

![Crystal clear emerald river flowing through rocks and trees in the Fara San Martino gorge](/images/explores/abruzzo/fara-san-martino-river.jpg)
*The water that comes out of this gorge ends up in your pasta. Not a bad origin story.*

Along the way, the riverbed is covered in stone cairns — hundreds of them, stacked by hikers who passed through. It's an unofficial tradition, and the effect is otherworldly.

![Hundreds of stone cairns lined up on the riverbed with gorge walls rising behind them](/images/explores/abruzzo/fara-san-martino-cairns.jpg)
*The cairn garden. Nobody planned it. It just happened.*

The gorge is a river bed. After heavy rain, it floods and becomes impassable. Check locally before setting out — the town's tourist office or any bar in the piazza will know.

## Pietracupa: The Village Under the Rock

Cross into Molise — barely 30 minutes from Fara San Martino — and you hit **Pietracupa**. It's a village of maybe 200 people built at the foot of a boulder so enormous it dominates the entire piazza. The church bell tower stands next to it like a polite neighbour trying not to stare.

![Pietracupa's piazza with the massive rock formation rising behind the village buildings and church bell tower under a dramatic sky](/images/explores/abruzzo/pietracupa-village.jpg)
*Pietracupa. The rock is not in the village. The village is around the rock.*

They call it "la piccola Betlemme" — the little Bethlehem — because the rock has a cave that's been used as a chapel and nativity scene for centuries. Inside, the stone is carved into a small church space. It's one of those places that makes you wonder how different life would be if you'd been born here instead.

<Accordion title="The cave church of Pietracupa">
The cave inside the rock has been a place of worship since at least the medieval period. Known as the Chiesa Rupestre, it was used by Samnite tribes, then Christians. Today it houses a permanent nativity scene and can be visited — ask at the bar in the piazza and someone will find the key. The acoustics inside the rock are extraordinary.
</Accordion>

## Why These Three

Abruzzo is full of pretty villages. What makes these three worth the drive is the contrast. A heart-shaped lake and a village that time forgot. A gorge so narrow it feels like the mountain is swallowing you. A rock so big a village just shrugged and built around it.

Allura — combine this loop with [Villalago](/explores/villalago-abruzzo) and [Rocca Calascio](/explores/rocca-calascio-abruzzo), and you've got a week in Abruzzo that beats most people's month in Tuscany.
