# Trabocchi Coast, Atri Calanchi & Sulmona: Sea, Badlands and Confetti in One Day

![Rocky Adriatic coastline with a trabocco fishing structure extending over turquoise water on wooden stilts](/images/explores/abruzzo/trabocchi-coast.jpg)
*Your first trabocco. Wooden legs in the sea, nets hanging, and probably a kitchen inside serving you the catch of the day.*

Most people think of Abruzzo as mountains. Bears, fortresses, gorges — all true. But drive east for an hour and the landscape flips. The Adriatic coast appears, and with it one of Italy's strangest and most beautiful stretches of shoreline: the **Costa dei Trabocchi**.

Then go inland. Twenty minutes from the coast, the earth splits into eroded clay canyons that look like they belong on Mars. And an hour further, a medieval town where they've been making sugar-coated almonds since the 15th century.

Coast, canyons, confetti. Only in Abruzzo.

<Callout type="insight" title="The other Abruzzo">
Everyone who visits Abruzzo heads for the mountains. The coast and the hills between the mountains and the sea are where you find the region's other personality — seafood, wine, clay badlands, and towns that have been quietly living well for centuries.
</Callout>

## Costa dei Trabocchi: Fishing Machines and Seafood Over Water

The trabocchi are ancient wooden fishing structures built on stilts over the Adriatic — spindly platforms of poles, planks, ropes, and nets that extend from the rocky shore out over the water. They look like something a mad engineer built during a storm, and they've been here since at least the 1600s.

![Close-up of a trabocco — a wooden fishing structure with platforms, nets, and a small cabin perched on stilts over the sea](/images/explores/abruzzo/trabocchi-closeup.jpg)
*Up close, a trabocco is pure engineering — every pole, rope, and plank has a purpose. The cabin? That's where they cook your lunch.*

Originally, fishermen used them to catch fish without having to go out to sea — important on a coast where storms come fast and boats are risky. A system of ropes and pulleys lowers nets into the water, and when the fish swim over, up they come.

Today, many trabocchi have been converted into restaurants. You walk out on a wooden gangway over the waves, sit at a table surrounded by sea on three sides, and eat whatever came up in the nets that morning. It's the most literal farm-to-table experience in Italy — except it's sea-to-table, and the table is literally on the sea.

<StatHighlight value="~30" label="Trabocchi still standing along the Costa dei Trabocchi — some dating to the 1600s" accent="pink" />

The coast also has a **cycling and walking path** — the Via Verde — that follows the old railway line along the shore. It's flat, paved, and runs for about 40 kilometres through olive groves, coves, and past trabocchi. Rent a bike in any of the small towns along the way.

<Callout type="tip" title="Book your trabocco dinner">
The restaurant trabocchi are hugely popular in summer. Book at least a week ahead for weekend dinners. Try Trabocco Punta Tufano or Trabocco Pesce Palombo for the full experience — fixed menus of whatever was caught that day.
</Callout>

## Atri Calanchi: Sunset Over a Moonscape

Twenty minutes inland from the coast, the gentle hills of olive groves and vineyards suddenly drop away into a landscape that looks like it was carved by a sculptor having a bad day. The **Calanchi di Atri** are badlands — deep clay ravines eroded by water over millennia into sharp ridges, pyramids, and gullies.

![Dramatic sunset over the Atri Calanchi — golden light hitting eroded clay ridges with distant mountains silhouetted against the sky](/images/explores/abruzzo/atri-calanchi-sunset.jpg)
*Sunset at the Calanchi. The clay turns gold, the shadows go deep, and you understand why people drive an hour just for this light.*

The nature reserve has walking trails along the rim, and the views at sunset are genuinely extraordinary. The clay changes colour as the light shifts — grey to gold to orange to deep purple. Behind you, the town of Atri sits on its hill. In front of you, the badlands drop away like the earth gave up.

Atri itself is worth a stop — a small medieval town with a cathedral that has frescoes by Andrea De Litio, one of Abruzzo's great Renaissance painters. And the local wine, Montepulciano d'Abruzzo, comes from vineyards growing right on the edge of the calanchi.

<Accordion title="How the calanchi formed">
The Calanchi di Atri are the result of water erosion on clay-rich soil over thousands of years. The clay absorbs water and then cracks as it dries, creating channels that deepen with each rainfall. The result is a landscape of sharp ridges and deep gullies that grows and changes every year. The reserve protects about 380 hectares of this terrain, and guided walks are available from the Atri visitor centre.
</Accordion>

## Sulmona: Ovid, Confetti and a Cloister That Stops Time

An hour inland from the coast, nestled in a valley between the Majella and Morrone mountains, sits **Sulmona** — a proper Italian town that happens to be spectacular.

![Medieval cloister with stone columns and arches surrounding an open courtyard — the Complesso della Santissima Annunziata in Sulmona](/images/explores/abruzzo/sulmona-cloister.jpg)
*The cloister of the Annunziata. The kind of place where you sit on a stone bench and forget what century it is.*

Sulmona is the birthplace of Ovid — the Roman poet who wrote the *Metamorphoses* — and the confetti capital of Italy. Not the paper kind. **Confetti** here means sugar-coated almonds, and Sulmona has been making them since the 1400s. The main street is lined with shops displaying confetti shaped into flowers, baskets, and elaborate sculptures. It's an art form.

![Display case full of decorated confetti — sugar-coated almond pastries shaped into characters and designs](/images/explores/abruzzo/sulmona-confetti.jpg)
*Sulmona confetti. These aren't just sweets — they're sculptural. Some of these shops have been here for centuries.*

But Sulmona isn't a tourist town. It's a working Italian city with a medieval core, excellent restaurants, a weekly market that takes over the piazza, and cultural weight that belies its size. The **Complesso della Santissima Annunziata** — part church, part museum, part cloister — is one of the most beautiful buildings in Abruzzo. Baroque interiors with Renaissance frescoes, crystal chandeliers, and a cloister so peaceful you'll want to move in.

![Baroque cathedral interior with ornate marble frame, religious painting, sculpted angels and cherubs](/images/explores/abruzzo/sulmona-cathedral.jpg)
*Inside the Annunziata. Baroque excess done right — every surface tells a story.*

<Callout type="tip" title="The perfect Abruzzo day trip">
Start at the Trabocchi coast for a morning walk and seafood lunch on a trabocco. Drive to Atri for a late afternoon walk along the calanchi rim at sunset. End in Sulmona for dinner and confetti shopping. It works, I've done it, and it's one of the best days you can have in Italy.
</Callout>

## The Region Nobody Mentions

Abruzzo doesn't market itself. It doesn't have an Amalfi Coast PR machine or a Tuscan wine lobby. It just sits there — mountains to sea in 90 minutes, medieval fortresses nobody visits, fishing platforms nobody photographs, and clay canyons nobody writes about.

Dai, that's changing. And it should. But go now, while the trabocchi still serve lunch to ten people instead of a hundred, and the calanchi sunset is yours and nobody else's.
