# Malta's First Travel Rule Ruling: A Tick-Box Just Cost a CASP 40% of the Loss

![Valletta skyline at dusk — the Maltese capital where the Arbiter for Financial Services sits and where the country's CASP supervisors are headquartered](/images/onchain/malta/valletta-skyline-placeholder.jpg)

For four months, every CASP in Europe has been operating under the recast Transfer of Funds Regulation without knowing what a regulator — or a court — would actually do with it. Now we have an answer. And it didn't come from Frankfurt, Paris, or Berlin. It came from a small tribunal sitting in Valletta.

The first decision in Malta applying Regulation (EU) 2023/1113 — the **TFR Recast**, the legal home of the crypto Travel Rule — has just landed. The headline finding is small in money terms (40% of a single retail loss) but enormous in implication: the Arbiter for Financial Services has effectively dragged Travel Rule compliance out of the AML supervisor's locked filing cabinet and into the open court of consumer protection.

That matters everywhere — but it matters first in Malta, where roughly two dozen MiCA-licensed and pre-MiCA VFA service providers operate from a 316 km² island. If you run one of them, or you advise one, this ruling resets the floor. Let me walk you through what actually happened, what the Arbiter said, and what you need to fix on Monday morning.

<Callout type="insight" title="The 30-second version">
A Maltese-licensed CASP let two USDT transfers leave a customer wallet to a self-hosted address controlled by a scammer. The customer had ticked "I own this wallet" on a self-declaration form. No verification was done. The Arbiter ruled the CASP breached its Travel Rule duties under the EBA Guidelines, that this triggered civil liability under the duty of care owed to clients, and awarded 40% of the loss. The decision has been formally notified to both the MFSA and the FIAU.
</Callout>

## The Facts

Two USDT transfers in January 2025. Both above €1,000. Sent from a wallet hosted at a Maltese-licensed VFA / MiCA service provider. Destination: a self-hosted external wallet on the Tron network — and, as it turned out, controlled by an online trading scam working a Malta-based victim.

The customer had completed a self-declaration form ticking the box that said "I own this wallet." The CASP didn't verify ownership any further before letting the transfers leave the platform.

The customer complained to the **Office of the Arbiter for Financial Services**, the Valletta-based statutory tribunal that handles consumer disputes against licensed financial service providers in Malta. The CASP's defence was, on paper, a clean legal argument: the Arbiter has no business adjudicating AML/CFT compliance — that sits squarely with the **Financial Intelligence Analysis Unit (FIAU)** under Chapter 373 of the Laws of Malta. Travel Rule obligations are AML obligations. End of jurisdiction.

The Arbiter didn't buy it.

## The Jurisdictional Move That Changes Everything

This is the part of the ruling that compliance teams need to read twice.

The Arbiter accepted, correctly, that he has **no power** to find that money laundering or terrorist financing took place, or to sanction a CASP for breaching the AML rulebook. That is the FIAU's exclusive remit, and the FIAU has shown over the last three years it is fully prepared to use it — its 2024 enforcement report alone records over €1 million in administrative penalties against Maltese-licensed entities.

But — and here is where it gets interesting — he drew a sharp line between **enforcing AML obligations** (FIAU) and **assessing whether a failure to comply with Travel Rule obligations caused harm to a financial consumer** (his job, under Cap. 555, the Arbiter for Financial Services Act).

In plain English: the Travel Rule is an AML rule, but breaching it is also a breach of the duty of care a CASP owes its clients. And duty-of-care breaches that cause loss are squarely within the Arbiter's mandate.

He anchored this to **Article 19(3) of Cap. 555**, the existing Court of Appeal (Inferior Jurisdiction) authority confirming that VFA service providers operating from Malta owe fiduciary obligations to their clients, and to **Article 66 of MiCA**, which codifies the duty to act honestly, fairly, professionally and in the best interests of clients.

The implication is brutal and clear: every Travel Rule failure is now a potential civil claim. A Maltese CASP can be in front of the FIAU in Pieta *and* in front of the Arbiter in Valletta for the same operational gap, on the same facts, at the same time.

## Why the Self-Declaration Defence Failed

![Placeholder — diagram of the Travel Rule ownership-verification flow under EBA Guidelines paragraphs 83–86, contrasting tick-box self-declaration against Satoshi test / signed message / wallet-attribution methods](/images/onchain/malta/travel-rule-verification-flow-placeholder.png)

The CASP's substantive defence rested on **paragraph 78 of the EBA's Travel Rule Guidelines**, which lets a CASP collect originator/beneficiary information directly from its customer where it cannot retrieve it via technical means. The CASP's reading: a signed self-declaration is enough.

The Arbiter rejected this, and he was right to.

He pointed to **paragraphs 83 to 86** of the same Guidelines — the paragraphs the FIAU formally adopted into the Maltese AML framework with effect from 30 December 2024 — which deal specifically with transfers above €1,000 to or from self-hosted addresses. Those paragraphs require the CASP to use **at least one verification method** — a Satoshi test, a signed message, manual signature in source code, or an equivalent technical approach — to be "fully satisfied" that the wallet is owned or controlled by the customer.

The CASP produced no evidence that any verification method had been applied. Worse, when the Arbiter ordered it to produce its internal Travel Rule policies and procedures, it didn't.

That last detail is the one that should keep CIOs up at night, in Malta and beyond. Not having the evidence is bad. Not having the policy is institutional.

## Why the Award Was 40%, Not 100%

The customer wasn't blameless and the Arbiter said so plainly. She had been coached by the scammer over Telegram, ticked the ownership box knowing she was being guided, and ignored multiple generic warnings the platform did serve up.

That contributory negligence is what reduced the award. But — and read this carefully — it did not eliminate it. **Contractual terms, generic warnings, and customer self-certification cannot absolve a CASP from its statutory Travel Rule obligations.** That sentence is the spine of the decision.

For anyone running consumer protection and risk teams in Malta: stop treating warning banners as a defence. They aren't.

## What You Need to Fix on Monday

This decision is going to be cited in every Travel Rule complaint Malta sees from now on, and it will travel beyond Malta because the underlying EBA Guidelines apply across the EU. Here is the short list of operational changes I would push through immediately.

**1. Kill the tick-box-only flow for self-hosted withdrawals above €1,000.**
You need a verification method on file before the transfer leaves. Satoshi test, signed message, or a wallet-attribution provider — pick one. Document which one was used, when, and by whom. A self-declaration on its own is now legally indefensible in Malta.

**2. Make sure your Travel Rule policy actually exists, in writing, with version control.**
The CASP in this case could not produce its own policies when the Arbiter asked. If you cannot, today, send me a PDF of your Travel Rule procedure with a sign-off date and an owner, you have the same exposure. Every Maltese CASP already has to produce this on request to the MFSA — now also to the Arbiter.

**3. Re-look at your whitelisting.**
Whitelisted addresses still need a verifiable ownership trail at the moment of whitelisting, plus periodic re-verification. A wallet whitelisted in 2023 with nothing but a tick-box should be unwhitelisted or re-verified before the next withdrawal.

**4. Build a scam-report response playbook.**
The moment a customer says "I think I've been scammed," the account should auto-flag for enhanced monitoring, the in-flight transfers paused, and any pending self-hosted withdrawals re-reviewed. The Arbiter explicitly criticised the absence of a quick risk re-classification in this case.

**5. Stop relying on warnings as a legal shield.**
Generic "are you sure?" pop-ups do not discharge the Travel Rule duty. They sit alongside it. In a complaint, they will be evidence of awareness, not of compliance.

## The Bigger Signal — for Malta and for Europe

The decision has been formally notified to both the MFSA and the FIAU. That is not a courtesy — it is a flare. The FIAU now has a documented case of a Maltese-licensed CASP failing the EBA Guidelines on a live transfer, and the MFSA now has a fiduciary-duty finding against a licensed entity sitting in its supervisory file in Mriehel.

Expect both to use it. Expect the next round of MFSA inspections to ask, very specifically, **"show me the verification method you used for self-hosted withdrawals above €1,000."** Expect the FIAU to fold this into its next thematic review — and given the FIAU's pattern of publishing anonymised findings, expect this case to appear in its 2026 annual report.

And expect more cases. The Arbiter sits in a system that has already heard a long line of crypto consumer complaints from Maltese retail users — Malta, despite its size, is one of the densest concentrations of licensed CASPs per capita in the EU. This is the first complaint applying the TFR Recast. It will not be the last.

The era of treating the Travel Rule as a paper exercise is over in Malta. It was always going to end. It just ended faster than most CASPs were ready for — and Malta, by being first to enforce it civilly, has set a tone the rest of Europe will now have to follow.
